# Product Launch Playbook
### An end-to-end framework for planning, executing, and measuring B2B SaaS product launches

---

> **A practical, repeatable playbook for PMMs who own launches end-to-end — from the first internal brief through to post-launch measurement and iteration.**

---

## What This Is

A product launch is the highest-stakes moment in a product marketer's calendar. Done well, it generates pipeline, accelerates adoption, and establishes your product's position in the market. Done poorly, it wastes months of product investment on a single forgettable week.

This playbook provides a structured approach to every stage of a B2B product launch with templates, checklists, and frameworks that can be adapted to launches of any size or complexity.

---

## Who This Is For

- Product marketers who own launches end-to-end
- PMMs at B2B SaaS, deep tech or AI product companies
- Marketing teams preparing for a first major product launch
- Cross-functional teams who need a shared launch framework

## Stage 1 — Launch Planning

### 1.1 Launch Tier Framework

Not every launch deserves the same resource and effort. Tiering your launches upfront prevents both under-investment in major moments and over-investment in minor ones.

| Tier | What it is | Examples | Timeline | Resource level |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1 — Major launch** | New product or significant market entry | New product line, new market, rebranded platform | 8–12 weeks | Full team, all channels, PR |
| **Tier 2 — Feature launch** | Significant new capability | Major new integration, new pricing tier, core feature | 4–6 weeks | Core PMM + growth, owned channels + some paid |
| **Tier 3 — Minor update** | Incremental improvement | UX improvement, minor feature, bug fix | 1–2 weeks | PMM only, owned channels |

**How to tier a launch:**

Ask these three questions:
```
1. Does this change the value proposition or target a new buyer?
   → Yes = likely Tier 1

2. Does this solve a top-3 customer pain point or unlock a new use case?
   → Yes = likely Tier 2

3. Is this an improvement to existing functionality with no new buyer impact?
   → Yes = likely Tier 3
```

### 1.3 Stakeholder Map

Identify every stakeholder involved in the launch and their role before execution begins. Misaligned stakeholders are the most common cause of launch delays.

| Stakeholder | Role | What they need | Input required | Sign-off required |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Product** | Feature scope, technical accuracy | Messaging review | Feature details, roadmap context | All external assets |
| **Sales** | Deal context, objection intelligence | Battle cards, demo script, objection handling | Top objections, competitive context | Sales assets |
| **Customer Success** | Existing customer impact | Comms plan for current customers | Customer impact assessment | Customer communications |
| **Legal** | Claims compliance | Asset review | Legal review window | External claims |
| **Leadership** | Strategic alignment | Narrative summary, projected impact | Positioning sign-off | Core positioning |
| **Growth / Demand Gen** | Campaign execution | Messaging brief, assets | Channel strategy input | Campaign assets |
| **Design** | Asset production | Messaging brief, copy | Design resource availability | Visual assets |

## Stage 2 — Positioning & Narrative

### 2.1 Launch Messaging Checklist

Before any asset goes into production, confirm the following are locked:

```
POSITIONING
- [ ] Positioning statement written and approved by Product
- [ ] Primary value proposition confirmed at all three levels
      (Feature → Benefit → Commercial outcome)
- [ ] Competitive differentiation updated for this launch
- [ ] Key objections identified and responses approved

PERSONAS
- [ ] Primary buyer persona confirmed
- [ ] Secondary personas identified
- [ ] Persona-specific messaging adapted for each stakeholder
- [ ] Buying committee messaging matrix complete

PROOF
- [ ] At least one customer proof point or case study available
- [ ] Key metrics confirmed and legally approved for external use
- [ ] Reference customers identified (for sales to use in conversations)
```

---

### 2.2 The Launch Narrative Arc

Every launch needs a narrative — not just a list of features. The strongest B2B launch narratives follow this structure:

```
ACT 1 — THE PROBLEM (make them feel seen)
└── Name the pain explicitly and specifically
    "Here's what's broken about the way [buyers] currently [do X]"
    Goal: The buyer reads this and thinks "that's exactly my problem"

ACT 2 — THE COST OF THE STATUS QUO (raise the stakes)
└── What does the problem cost — in time, money, risk, or opportunity?
    Goal: Establish urgency — the cost of doing nothing

ACT 3 — THE SOLUTION (introduce your product as the enabler)
└── How you solve it — specific, evidenced, buyer-centric
    Goal: The buyer sees exactly how their life improves

ACT 4 — THE PROOF (remove the risk)
└── Who else has solved it this way — and what happened?
    Goal: Transfer conviction from you to a credible third party

ACT 5 — THE CALL TO ACTION (make the next step obvious)
└── One clear, low-friction next step
    Goal: Convert attention into action
```

### 3.2 Asset Quality Standards

Every asset produced for a launch should meet these standards before approval:

```
CLARITY
- [ ] Can be understood without prior knowledge of the product
- [ ] No unexplained jargon or acronyms
- [ ] Single most important message is immediately clear

CONSISTENCY
- [ ] Reflects approved messaging framework
- [ ] Uses approved product names and terminology
- [ ] Consistent tone and voice with other launch assets

ACCURACY
- [ ] All claims approved by Product
- [ ] All statistics sourced and approved for external use
- [ ] Competitor references reviewed by Legal (if included)

EFFECTIVENESS
- [ ] Clear single CTA
- [ ] Proof point included
- [ ] Persona-appropriate — written for the right reader
```

---

### 3.3 Sales Enablement Checklist

Sales must be enabled before the first external asset goes live. A launch that goes to market before sales is ready generates inbound that doesn't convert.

```
PRE-LAUNCH SALES ENABLEMENT
- [ ] Updated pitch deck with new product narrative
- [ ] One-pager for leave-behind
- [ ] Battle card updated for competitive objections
- [ ] Demo script / talk track reflecting new messaging
- [ ] FAQ document covering top 10 expected questions
- [ ] Reference customers identified and briefed

SALES TRAINING SESSION
- [ ] Walk through updated positioning and messaging
- [ ] Demo the product / new feature
- [ ] Role-play top objections using new battle card
- [ ] Confirm sales team questions are answered
- [ ] Sales team sign-off that they're ready to pitch

POST-LAUNCH SALES SUPPORT
- [ ] PMM available for first week of launch for live questions
- [ ] Slack channel or equivalent for real-time sales <> PMM comms
- [ ] Week 1 debrief scheduled
```

### 5.3 Attribution Framework

Understanding what drove results is as important as the results themselves. Use this model to attribute pipeline to launch activity:

```
FIRST TOUCH
└── What was the buyer's first interaction with the launch?
    (Identifies which channel generated awareness)

LAST TOUCH
└── What was the buyer's last interaction before converting?
    (Identifies which asset or channel drove the decision)

MULTI-TOUCH
└── Which combination of touchpoints appeared in winning journeys?
    (Identifies the most effective sequence — most useful for future launches)
```

**Practical approach for lean teams:** If full multi-touch attribution isn't set up, capture first touch and last touch at minimum. Add a "How did you hear about us?" field to your demo request form — imperfect but directionally valuable.

## Core Principles

**1. Nail the brief before touching an asset**
Every hour spent on a misaligned asset is wasted. The brief is the highest-leverage document in a launch — treat it as such.

**2. Enable sales before you go external**
A launch that generates inbound before sales is ready destroys pipeline. Sales enablement is not a launch deliverable — it's a launch prerequisite.

**3. Launch day is not the finish line**
The launch moment is the beginning of a measurement and iteration cycle, not the end of a production cycle. The PMMs who drive the most impact treat day 0 as the start, not the destination.

**4. One launch objective, measured relentlessly**
Launches with five objectives achieve none of them. Pick one primary metric, set a target, and measure it daily in week one.

**5. Document everything**
Every launch produces learnings that make the next one better. A post-launch review that isn't written down and shared is a learning that dies with the project.
